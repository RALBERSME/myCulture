You must not understand this life.


You must not understand this life,
Then everyday will be like a party.
Simply let every day be
like a child who, passing by,
receives many flowers from the wind.

She doesn't collect or save them,
that doesn't even occur to her.
They are lost from her hair
where they were so happily arranged
uand in the sweet years of her youth,
she reaches out to embrace the new.


Rainer Maria Rilke, 1909

Rainer Maria Rilke



Rainer Maria Rilke was born on December 4, 1875, in Prague (then Austria-Hungary) and died on December 29, 1926, in Valmont, Switzerland. He grew up in a middle-class, artistically inclined environment. His father was a civil servant, and his mother a passionate musician. Rilke's innate empathy for language and art became evident early on. Despite changing schools several times (including the military academy in Linz, the Vienna State Gymnasium, and later studies in Prague), he developed a deep fascination with poetry, music, and philosophy. His path led him from Prague and Munich to Paris, where French poetry and culture strongly influenced him.

Rilke was not a conventional academic, but rather sought his own path through intensive self-study, letters, encounters, and travels. He initially worked as a civil servant and art dealer's assistant, but pursued his own poetic education in dialogue with mentors such as Lou Andreas-Salomé, who profoundly influenced him intellectually. His thinking was characterized by existential reflection, spirituality, and a calm, precise language that sought to capture the sacred in the everyday. His literary work was driven by a constant search for the right word and form.

Among his most important works is "The Prague Window" (1902, an early lyrical sketch in which the poet's gaze is directed at the soul and its surroundings). The text reflects the search for meaning in the tension between the inner world and outer reality. Also famous are "The Notebooks of Malte Laurids Brigge" (1910, prose fragments that place the inner life of a sensitive writer in a modern, existential perspective). This is about identity, transience, and art as a means of understanding. In the "Duino Elegies" (1903-1908, final version published in 1923), he wrote deeply poetic meditations on death, desire, and transcendence. Rilke develops a metaphysical view of existence that understands art as a path to truth.

Rilke's language is characterized by clarity, rhythmic flow, and a subtle finesse that appeals to both contemporary and past readers. Profound existential questions are addressed, such as love, loneliness, death, and the search for meaning – universal themes that still resonate and inspire reflection today. Rilke combines aesthetic form with metaphysical depth. His texts invite meditative reading and promote an awareness of art as a path to knowledge.

Rilke's works are multifaceted. The works mentioned above mark significant turning points in his oeuvre and are particularly suitable for gaining an overview of his thought and poetic power.
