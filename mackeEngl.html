August Macke



(born January 4, 1887, died September 26, 1914) was one of the central German painters of Expressionism and a formative figure of the artist group « Der Blaue Reiter » (The Blue Rider). He was born in Meseritz (now Międzyrzecz, Poland) and died in the field hospital of Monatruet, near the Flanders front during the First World War.

Education and early influences: Macke initially studied commercial art in Aachen, later painting at the Grand Ducal Saxon School of Art in Dresden. From 1906-1907 he studied with Heinrich C. von Zügel in Karlsruhe, where French Impressionism and the newer movements in France had a lasting influence on him. From 1907 to 1908 he traveled to Paris, where he was influenced by the works of Degas, Monet, and Cézanne, as well as the lightness of the Divisionist colors.

Artistic development: In the years before the First World War, Macke developed a color-intensive, luminous style that marks the transition from Impressionism to later Expressionism. Typical characteristics include clear contours, flat areas of color, and an optimistic, often summery, light atmosphere. He collaborated with the « Der Blaue Reiter » circle and maintained contact with Wassily Kandinsky, Franz Marc, and Paul Klee. In 1913, Macke undertook a significant trip to Tunis, where the contrast of sunlight, the Mediterranean color palette, and the exotic architecture inspired him to new coloristic approaches. The experiences of this trip strongly influenced his later painting, particularly in terms of lighting and color dynamics.

Style and motifs: Macke's works often depict street scenes, outdoor scenes, and portraits with a harmonious, almost decorative visual language. He experimented with a reduced color palette that nevertheless unfolds with intense luminosity. Typical of his work are melodic sketch lines, a quiet melancholy, and a sense of balance between nature and humanity.

Inspirations and influences: In addition to the French artists, he was also influenced by the landscape painters of North Rhine-Westphalia and the Art Nouveau aesthetic. The group Der Blaue Reiter (The Blue Rider), whose members Macke was closely associated with, pursued the goal of creating inwardly liberating, spiritually charged art, beyond naturalistic representation.

Legacy: Macke's short career – less than a decade as an independent painter – left behind a rich, colorful visual world that marks the bridge from Impressionism to Expressionist color theory. His death at the front in 1914 prematurely removed him from the art scene, but his work continues to resonate as a key expression of early Expressionism: the enthusiasm for color, light, and the striving for a transcendent yet immediately experienceable reality.
